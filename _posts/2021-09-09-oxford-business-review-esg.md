*Co-authored with Ranusha de Silva and published in the Oxford Business Review.*

COVID-19 lockdowns caused fossil carbon dioxide emissions to decline by an estimated 2.4 billion tonnes in 2020. Whether this was the planet's way of intervening may never be known — but the pandemic and global warming alike are crises that will continue if we do not act together. Without cooperation, new strains develop, and the locked-down environment compounds the rapid onset of disaster.

In a similar way, businesses must work in unison to address Environment, Social and Governance (ESG) issues. But unlike COVID, root causes are diffuse, showing up instead as increases in flooding, famine, and fires. We all know that economic development without concern for ESG will only harm the communities and planet we inhabit.

## The business case is not a concession

What many businesses miss is that ESG alignment is not a trade-off against profitability — it is frequently a multiplier of it. Whilst groups like Greenpeace and Extinction Rebellion draw attention through direct action, driving a global ESG and responsible investing programme may be a more structured and profitable path toward the same goals.

Even if you are sceptical of the environmental framing, investing in ESG can produce traditional business advantages:

- **Lowered Total Cost of Operations (TCO):** Energy efficiency, waste reduction, and sustainable supply chains directly reduce operational costs.
- **Improved scalability:** Sustainable processes are more resilient and scalable than those dependent on finite or volatile resources.
- **Employee morale:** Purpose-driven organisations attract and retain talent more effectively, particularly among younger engineers and product teams.
- **Stakeholder engagement:** Investors, customers, and regulators increasingly weight ESG performance in their decisions.

## The innovation angle

For those of us who think in terms of technology and product development, the ESG challenge is fundamentally an innovation challenge. Where are the unsustainable processes in your business? What technology can reduce waste, enable circular economy models, or substitute renewable for non-renewable inputs?

This is precisely the frame I brought to the UNDP SDG launch in 2015 — that innovators have a specific responsibility to apply their skills to sustainability challenges, not just to growth.
