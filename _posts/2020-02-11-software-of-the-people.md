ICT is increasingly lubricating the democratic process and has tremendous potential to enhance it further. Yet much has also been said about the exploitation of software — Facebook, Twitter — to spread disinformation and distort the public opinion of an electorate. We sometimes lose sight of what software *can* do when built in the open: improve transparency, bridge communities, and give more power to the people.

The argument I want to make is that software built for government — and particularly election management software — should be built as Free and Open Source Software. Not primarily for cost reasons, but because of a structural alignment between the values of democracy and the principles of open source.

I take my title from Lincoln's Gettysburg Address, modified slightly:

> *Software owned of the people, built by the people, for the people.*

## Principle 1: Software owned of the people

Most software you use belongs to one company. You never own it — you lease it. The company decides how it works and what it does with your data. Government software, particularly that which runs elections and public policy, should belong to the people. Any citizen should be able to obtain a copy of the code and inspect it, or have someone inspect it on their behalf. Open source copyright licences make this a legal right, not a favour.

Government software is also built with taxpayers' money. It is a public good and citizens have every right to inspect it.

## Principle 2: Software built by the people

Government software should not be built by one company, one ethnic group, or one political party. It should be built by a diverse group of interested parties — any citizen with the required skills should be welcome to participate as a public service.

Open source welcomes open participation. But unrestricted participation can lead to analysis-paralysis. What has proven to work in successful open source communities (Apache, Debian) is a meritocracy of doers: everyone is welcome to participate, but those who contribute the most earn greater decision-making power. This is the political equivalent of earned authority rather than appointed authority.

## Principle 3: Software for the people

The entire electorate should be invited to test the software, provide feedback, and audit decisions transparently. Open source gives users — citizens — legal rights to inspect the code, not at a surface level but at full depth. You, or a developer you trust, can take it apart and examine it for anything that concerns you.

## Security through transparency, not obscurity

Many assume that exposing source code makes election software vulnerable to attack. This is the same reasoning that justified secret ballot-counting. In software, it is backwards: just as vulnerabilities can be found, they can also be *fixed* publicly, and the diversity of the open source review process is a far stronger security model than private code review by a single vendor.

Linux, the most deployed operating system on Earth, is open source. Its security record is substantially better than proprietary alternatives — precisely because of the breadth and rigour of open community review.

## The governance of software for governance

Open source governance models vary. On one end: commercial open source (Red Hat, Google, AWS). On the other: pure community meritocracies (Debian, Apache). The latter maps most naturally to government software — transparent, voted on, documented. Decisions on roadmap, architecture, policy, and even specific lines of code are made in public, auditable forums.

Free and Open Source Software has its roots in freedom. It is naturally aligned with the development of government software. With such software, all citizens have inalienable freedoms protected by copyright law. And one wonders whether government itself might run more effectively if it operated more like a well-governed open source project.
