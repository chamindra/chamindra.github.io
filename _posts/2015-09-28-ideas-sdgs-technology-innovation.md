*Presented at a UNDP-organised forum on the day of the Sustainable Development Goals launch, September 2015.*

I was invited to speak on how technology and innovation can make a difference in accelerating the 18 SDGs and their 169 targets. The full presentation is on [SlideShare](https://www.slideshare.net/chamindra/slideshelf) — below are the key ideas.

## The IPAT equation and technology's role

The IPAT equation describes environmental impact as a function of Population × Affluence × Technology. Technology (T) typically expresses consumption and emissions per unit of economic activity. The good news: technology is the lever over which innovators have the most direct control.

But technology can also influence A (affluence/consumption per person) through social engagement — nudging behaviour, surfacing the environmental cost of choices, and enabling better decisions at the individual level.

## Connecting the dots from 169 targets to daily action

The SDGs suffer from a translation problem. Eighteen goals and 169 targets are meaningful to policymakers and NGOs, but largely abstract to the individuals whose aggregate daily choices will determine whether the goals are met.

The innovation opportunity is in building the bridge from macro-target to micro-action — from "Goal 13: Climate Action" to "planting this tree earns 2 SDG points" or "choosing this product reduced emissions by X grams."

## Gamified mobile engagement

Imagine a Nike Fuel for sustainable living — a mobile experience that rewards verifiable sustainable actions with points, streaks, and community recognition. For children, something closer to Plants vs Zombies: actions in the physical world (planting trees, reducing energy, composting) translate into game progress.

The validation challenge is real. How do you verify that the tree was planted, or that the energy was saved? Third-party validation, community witness, and hardware integration (smart meters, etc.) each offer partial answers.

## Embedding sustainability into online commerce

Amazon already shows nutrition labels on food. The next frontier is SDG ratings for consumer goods — validated by bodies like the UN Global Compact or equivalent third parties — surfaced at the point of purchase. Wouldn't knowing that a product was built without toxic materials, by a company with verified labour standards, influence your decision?

The data is increasingly available. The interface layer — connecting sustainability data to the moment of consumer decision — is where innovators can have impact now.

## The call to action

The SDGs are a global coordination problem. Technology is one of the few levers that can operate at the speed and scale the problem requires. For engineers, designers, and product people: the SDGs are your design brief. The targets are your requirements. Innovation is the delivery mechanism.
