I was invited to present at the UNDP Social Good Summit on the same day the UN officially launched the Sustainable Development Goals. The timing made the stakes feel very immediate — not an abstract policy discussion but a live question of how technology communities should respond to what was just committed to globally.

The central argument in the presentation is that STI deployed without the SDG framework as a constraint can cause more harm than good. The IPAT equation captures this: Impact = Population × Affluence × Technology. Technology reduces the T factor, but unconstrained affluence and consumption can still overwhelm any efficiency gains. The opportunity is to use STI to address all three variables simultaneously.

Key ideas from the talk (slides 9–12 in particular):

- **Connecting the 169 SDG targets to individual action.** The SDGs are comprehensive but abstract to most people. The missing layer is a translation mechanism that maps everyday choices — what you buy, how you commute, how you eat — to specific targets.

- **Gamified mobile engagement.** I proposed a model similar to Nike Fuel: a points system where sustainable actions earn SDG credits, tiered by audience from children (think Plants vs. Zombies mechanics) to adults. Validating the actions is the hard problem — it would require third-party verification or sensor integration.

- **SDG ratings at the point of purchase.** If Amazon surfaced UN Global Compact compliance or Eco-label ratings alongside product listings, the aggregate effect on consumption decisions could be significant. The data infrastructure exists; the will to embed it in commerce does not yet.

- **Corporate STI aligned to SDGs.** Companies that frame their innovation roadmaps around specific SDG targets gain both a filtering lens for investment decisions and a credible ESG narrative — not as a compliance exercise but as a strategic constraint that improves the quality of innovation choices.

The talk was part of a broader summit session on technology and the post-2015 agenda. The context — the SDGs having just been formally adopted — gave the discussion an immediacy that made the abstract frameworks feel more urgent than they usually do in conference settings.
